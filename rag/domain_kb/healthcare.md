# Healthcare Glossary

## BMI (Body Mass Index)
Body Mass Index is calculated as weight in kilograms divided by the square of height in meters (kg/m^2).
Standard categories defined by the World Health Organization:
- Underweight: BMI < 18.5
- Normal weight: BMI 18.5 to 24.9
- Overweight: BMI 25.0 to 29.9
- Obese class I: BMI 30.0 to 34.9
- Obese class II: BMI 35.0 to 39.9
- Obese class III (morbid obesity): BMI >= 40.0
Clinical relevance: BMI >= 40 is associated with significantly elevated risk of cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, and certain cancers. Patients in this range typically warrant clinical attention rather than being treated as data quality artifacts. BMI alone does not distinguish lean mass from fat mass.

## HbA1c (Glycated Hemoglobin)
HbA1c reflects average blood glucose levels over the prior two to three months and is the standard biomarker for diabetes diagnosis and monitoring. Reported as a percentage of total hemoglobin.
ADA-defined diagnostic thresholds:
- Normal: HbA1c < 5.7%
- Prediabetes: HbA1c 5.7% to 6.4%
- Diabetes: HbA1c >= 6.5%
- Poor glycemic control: HbA1c > 9.0%
Clinical relevance: Patients with HbA1c >= 6.5% should be flagged for diabetes management; values above 9% indicate uncontrolled diabetes warranting therapeutic intervention.

## Systolic Blood Pressure
Systolic blood pressure measures arterial pressure during ventricular contraction, expressed in mmHg.
ACC/AHA categories:
- Normal: < 120 mmHg
- Elevated: 120 to 129 mmHg
- Stage 1 hypertension: 130 to 139 mmHg
- Stage 2 hypertension: 140 to 179 mmHg
- Hypertensive crisis: >= 180 mmHg
Clinical relevance: Sustained readings >= 180 mmHg constitute a medical emergency. Chronic hypertension is a leading risk factor for stroke, heart failure, and chronic kidney disease.

## Diastolic Blood Pressure
Diastolic blood pressure measures arterial pressure between heartbeats, expressed in mmHg.
- Normal: < 80 mmHg
- Stage 1 hypertension: 80 to 89 mmHg
- Stage 2 hypertension: 90 to 119 mmHg
- Hypertensive crisis: >= 120 mmHg
Isolated diastolic hypertension is more common in younger adults; isolated systolic hypertension predominates in older adults.

## Age
Patient age in years. Pediatric (<18), adult (18-64), older adult (>=65). Many vital sign reference ranges are age-stratified; what is normal in a 25-year-old may indicate pathology in an 80-year-old and vice versa.
